Project Description

What is BerryVax

At iGEM ITESO, we created BerryVax as an integrated biotechnological solution for fungal pests and water scarcity in berry fields. This is possible by the enhancement of Bacillus subtilis, a world-spread bacteria present in the soil, to make it overproduce surfactin, which is a biosurfactant (Coutte et al., 2015). This substance permits B. subtilis to act as a pythovaccine, which means it works as a vaccine for plants, since it allows their defense system to stay alert, avoiding pathogens to enter it, while also improving surface wettability and fruit quality (Salehi et al., 2010; Coutte et al., 2015). Adding to this, when BerryVax is exposed to fusaric acid, a compound made by fungi, it will start producing chitinase, a compound that degrades glucans, chitin and fatty acids present in pathogenic fungi that attack berry plants (Swain, 2008; Sandoval-Chávez et al., 2015). This is done by degrading via the production of chitinase. This way, BerryVax, will have two different action modes: the preventive and the corrective one.

Why is BerryVax?

Defining our topic

Synthetic biology can be applied to uncountable scenarios, which is why we decided to have an open perspective about the issue we chose to work on. For this reason, each one of the team members worked on a researching about a local or global issue that they were interested in, like teen pregnancies, berry pests, depression, diabetes, and electronic waste. Each team member developed an empathy map, as well as an archetype for the people that would need the proposed solution. After presenting all the possible topics within the team, we voted for our favourites, and the top five were turned into investigation lines. After doing our research on each one of them, we reached out to stakeholders, to help us validate if our concept of the situation was really a problem or not. Later, we presented our investigation results to different professors, as well as to Ana Ruth Quintero, former iGEM ITESO team member, and iGEM LATAM Ambassador. Thanks to their feedback, we decided where we could make a greater impact, which lead us to choose treating pests in berry fields.

Getting involved

Our state, Jalisco, is one of the main berry producers and exporters of the country. According to ANEBERRIES’s general director, Juan José Flores García (personal communication, May 23rd, 2022), there’s more than 34,000 hectares of berry fields just in Jalisco, which currently give employment to more than 500,000 people. And, according to Lagunes-Fortiz et al., (2020), from 2003 to 2016, there has been a 21.8% increase in the Mexican berries production, which indicates that this field will continue to grow. However, as we learned more about the berry production context here in Jalisco by talking with different stakeholders, we discovered that pests were only a fraction of their problems, which is why we decided to amplify our solution’s reach by targeting both water scarcity and pests. Thanks to the multiple interactions we had with our stakeholders, we were able to design BerryVax, a solution that will satisfy their main needs: keeping their crops healthy, organic, and productive. How we developed BerryVax

We found that fungal pests were getting each time more harmful for berry producers, since a lot of them, like pathogenic Fusarium oxysporum, infect the plants at its roots, making its early detection very hard. This fungus is considered a hemibiotroph. First, it uses the plant as a host to take advantage of its healthy tissues and later, it starts degrading its immune system and tissues by producing toxins and virulence factors to use the plant’s nutrients (Lyons et al., 2015).

Even though in some of the crops we visited the plants were regularly checked for pathogens, which prevented the infection from being spread across the field, it was still very difficult to treat the sicknesses without using traditional methods, like agrochemicals, which can not only harm the ecosystem and the living beings in it, but can also affect the chances of producers to sell their fruits at a good price due to the growing demand for organic berries.

Taking this into account, we decided to focus on treating fungal pests in berries through Synthetic Biology and, after a lot of research, it was found that a way to do it was via the overproduction of surfactin, a biosurfactant capable of providing protection to the plant in both pre- and post-harvest conditions (Valenzuela et al., 2020), which makes it even more useful for producers, since they had told us there were losses in both of these stages. Furthermore, when looking for microorganisms who produced this compound, we found our chassis, Bacillus subtilis, a bacterium that can be found in the soil. Adding to this, B. subtilis also promotes plant growth, enhances biotic stress tolerance, and improves surface wettability (Radhakrishan et al., 2017; Salehi et al., 2010).

However, since BerryVax would need to be released into the environment to be used as a biocontrol method, a biocontainment mechanism was needed. Therefore, a suicide switch based on team iGEM LMU Munich (2012) one was included.
