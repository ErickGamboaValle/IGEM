Project modeling

Project modeling

Knowing how our product works is of great importance because it will give us the tools to estimate the scope of our project. We created a model in which we can see the how the life cycle of the Bacillus subtilis will behave as well as the amount of surfactin it will produce. This will give us important information on the quantities need to have an efficient biocontrol process against Fusarium oxysporum in berry crops.

Important note: This simulation was made as if the bacteria didn’t have any genetic modifications.

We started by looking in the literature where we found a paper investigation of “Kinetic study and characterization of surfactin production by Bacillus subtilis UFPEDA 438 using sugarcane molasses as carbon source” by Patricia Rocha et al. which was used as a reference for kinetic data such as quantities of biomass, substrate and product used in the investigation as well as the biomass and product yield. We took the data and with the help of MATLAB we created a program that by using a function of minimum and maximum values (fmincon), it was possible to determine kinetic parameters including the specific growth rate (umax), the growth rate constant (Ks), the maintenance of the substate (ms), the alpha and beta (α β).

Figure 1. Graphic from where kinetic data was taken, and Kinetic parameters obtained using fmincon. (Patrícia Maria Rocha 2020)

Once the previous data was obtained, we proceeded to use Ordinary Differential Equations (ODE) for a batch procedure to get the data of calculated biomass, substrate, and product formation. Later on, we graphed all the data obtained so we could compare the experimental and the calculated data as shown in Figure 3. The results behaved differently in each situation, and we can conclude that the reason of this is because in the simulation a lot of variables were not considered.

Figure 2. Calculated vs experimental data

In the paper of investigation used as a reference the authors said that the best growth conditions for the bacteria where at 36°C with an agitation of 200 rpm and aeration of 0.4 vvm. With this information we created a second simulation in which we wanted to see the amount of biomass we could create inside a bioreactor at industrial scale and the amount of surfactin these bacteria could produce in a natural scenario.

We used a 350 m3 bioreactor with x g/l of biomass, x g/L of substrate and x g/l of surfactin as initial conditions. The bioreactor worked at 36°C, 200 rpm and 4 vvm for 72 hours using a fed batch with a synodal function adding 50 g/L of substrate with the purpose of having a more efficient process. The simulation gives the option to use different types of impellers so the user can see with which ones the best result is obtained, the options are Rushton, marine, turbine, hydrofoil and the combination of Rushton and turbine. With the help of ODE, we were able to simulate the growth getting the best results using two Rushton impellers, the maximum cell growth was of approximately 12.5 g/L at 24h with a substrate consumption of the 0.14% and a product formation of 2.5 g/L of surfactin. As shown in the graphs, the volume rises continuously given the fact that we are working with a fed batch system and, it is also shown how the oxygen behaves, the consumption and transfer rates.
